Preamble

I could write a novel about the causes and effects in my life that moved me towards a career in software development. However, I can name three formative experiences/things that form a loose outline:

  1. Building and programming multiple Lego Mindstorms robots in elementary school;
  2. Participating on an FRC team 5690 SubZero Robotics (see more on this below);
  3. My father's love for asking questions, figuring out math, and reading Wikipedia.

SubZero Robotics

I joined the high school robotics team on the recommendation of a friend, and this resulted in my first contact with real code. Real, ugly, functional code written by students just about as clueless as clueless comes (one of these students, a friend, is now a phenomenal programmer out in the private tech world). I learned the pain of trying to interpret poorly-documented code, though it wasn’t until later that I learned what good documentation looked like!

We managed to build some great code, such as a system that interfaced with laser light detectors and conveyors to automatically manage a robot’s intake system. We also wrote computer vision code interfacing with a Limelight camera and the robot’s drivetrain to perform auto-alignment to game targets; this required a great deal of PID tuning and latency cutting, while the target finding system was largely plug and play minus a few vision-parameter tunings.

I had an awful time learning C++ as a first language; I feel that I only truly learned to program when I entered college and purposefully studied Python. I wasted a lot of time trying to glean information off of esoteric C++ documentation instead of a well built tutorial designed for beginners. But I felt so much pressure to simply get functional code on the robot when it was needed, and failed to see the excellent value in spending “work time” on learning.

This only scratches the surface of the many things I picked up on the robotics team, least of all leadership and communication techniques. But those are stories for another place (the miscellany page).

Where I'm (planning on) going

Aside from Scotland, home of my ancestors I am chiefly interested in lower level development, whether that be embedded systems or OS/kernel development. Years of living with engineers and robots has drilled into me a certain love for the physical applications of the digital world.

All Hail C!

I really love programming in C; it gives me the high of feeling like I'm in control, while also being miles less painful than assembly. It's also mostly non-paradigmatic, not quite Object Oriented but certainly not functional. Instead, it provides an abstract interface to your machine's resources that is unbiased and transparent.

Obviously, C isn't suited to all computing tasks; for everyday problem solving, I'm likely to default to Python (another favorite of mine). But Python can never scratch that certain need-to-know-what-and-why itch of mine like C can.
